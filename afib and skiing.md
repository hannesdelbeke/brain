[This article](https://www.heart.org/en/news/2019/08/26/study-of-skiers-holds-surprises-about-afib-stroke-and-intense-exercise) is a news summary from the American Heart Association about a study of long-distance cross-country skiers and what it found about AFib ([[atrial fibrillation]]), [[stroke]], and intense exercise.

### 1. Some top male skiers had more AFib

Men with the best race performances had a **higher risk of developing AFib** than non-skiers. This supports earlier findings that **very high lifelong endurance training may slightly increase AFib risk** in some people.

### 2. But they did **not** have more strokes

This was the surprising part.

Even though some skiers had more AFib, they **did not have a higher stroke risk**. Male skiers with AFib had **lower stroke risk after diagnosis** than non-skiers with AFib, and lower death from stroke as well.

### 3. Women looked even better

Female skiers were:

- less likely to develop AFib
- less likely to have a stroke

compared with women who didn’t ski.

- Regular exercise is strongly beneficial.
- Even vigorous exercise is usually good overall.
- Only a small minority doing **extreme multi-hour training for years** may see increased AFib risk.

## compare snowboarding
wonder if this is similar for [[snowboarding]].
> Possibly somewhat similar in overall cardiovascular benefit, but not identical, because snowboarding stresses the body differently than cross-country skiing.

Cross-country skiing is usually:
- sustained endurance exercise for long periods
- high aerobic demand
- repetitive rhythmic movement

Snowboarding is more often:
- intermittent bursts of effort
- balance, coordination, leg strength
- stop/start activity with chairlift rest periods (at resorts)
- less continuous aerobic load unless hiking/backcountry/freestyle laps

So it may provide **less pure endurance stimulus** than cross-country skiing, but more balance/power/core work.

The increased AFib signal in some endurance athletes is usually linked to **many years of very high-volume endurance training** (marathons, elite cycling, cross-country ski racing, etc.).

Typical recreational snowboarding probably **would not be expected to carry the same AFib pattern**, because it usually isn’t hours of sustained high-output aerobic training year-round.
